## feature-surface

Staxis is a broad, surprisingly deep hotel-operations platform — far more mature than a typical vibe-coded MVP. The web app (Next.js on Vercel) is organized as ~20 product sections, each a tab-orchestrator page that delegates to dozens of well-factored components. Beyond the UI there are three real "engines": (1) a per-hotel computer-use agent (cua-service) that stays logged into the PMS 24/7 and ingests live feeds into a 15-table pms_* schema; (2) a Python ML service with Bayesian cold-start, XGBoost-quantile demand/supply models, a Monte-Carlo headcount optimizer, and shadow-mode auto-rollback; (3) an in-app AI agent (chat + voice) with ~35 real registered tools spanning rooms, queries, management, reports, complaints, compliance, lost-and-found, financials, knowledge (RAG), PMS feeds, and persistent memory. Supporting systems include a 12-rule housekeeping rules-engine (VIP arrival, eco-stay, tight-turnaround, deep-clean rotation, etc.) that auto-generates room tasks, a sick-callout auto-redistribution engine, an 11-report catalog with scheduled bilingual email + AI summaries + CSV export, an engineering-compliance engine with anomaly detection and auto-actions, invoice OCR for inventory, vector-embedding knowledge search, per-hotel capability access control, 45 cron jobs, and 243 DB migrations. CRITICAL METHODOLOGY NOTE: the local Desktop checkout is 298 commits behind origin/main and shows dozens of core files (the entire agent tools catalog, agent llm/context/cost-controls, most tests) as locally DELETED — they exist and are live on origin/main. All findings below are based on origin/main (the live product), not the stale local tree. The two most maturity-relevant caveats: the flagship /feed "decision inbox" is explicitly built on sample data (real wiring is Phase 2+), and the main dashboard's financial KPIs/charts are synthetic and shown ONLY on demo properties — real hotels correctly see "no history yet."

## cua-pms-pipeline

The CUA/PMS pipeline is Staxis's most novel and defensible asset, and it is genuinely sophisticated — far more than a typical "vibe-coded" scraper. It is a per-hotel persistent Playwright + Chromium browser (one BrowserContext per hotel, deployed one-Fly-machine-per-hotel) that stays logged into the PMS 24/7, polls feeds every 30s±10s, and writes normalized data into a 15-table pms_* schema. There are two distinct paths: (1) a CHEAP deterministic REPLAY path (recipe-runner / extractors / generic-table-writer — no AI, ~60-90s/poll, $0 in tokens) that does all routine data collection, and (2) an EXPENSIVE AI MAPPING path (mapper.ts, 91KB) where Claude vision drives the browser to "learn" where 15 categories of data live in a brand-new PMS, producing a versioned, shared-per-PMS-family "knowledge file" (recipe). The architecture is correct and well-layered: knowledge files are versioned with draft→active→deprecated→quarantined states and exactly-one-active-per-family enforced at the DB; the mapper learns once per PMS family and every hotel on that family reuses it. The whole thing reflects real adversarial-review discipline (cost caps, redaction, idempotency, abort signals, fail-open/fail-safe decisions are all deliberate and commented). The HONEST maturity caveat: this has been proven end-to-end on essentially ONE PMS (Choice Advantage), the only family with a seeded knowledge file (migration 0203), the only family with parsers (parsers/ca.ts), and the from-scratch vision mapper has a documented history of struggling (per memory: CA full-learn found 7/20 targets even on Opus, re-maps failed multiple times burning ~$11-20). The 6 "active" feeds work; 9 of the 15 schema tables are intentionally empty. So the schema and machinery are built for many PMS families and 15 feed types, but real-world proof is one PMS and ~6 feeds.

**What it can do today:**
- Persistent per-hotel browser logged into the PMS 24/7 (session-driver.ts): boots Chromium with saved storageState/cookies, verifies login via success-selector probe, re-logs-in on expiry (clears cookies, replays login steps), saves fresh session state back to Supabase
- Two-path design: deterministic REPLAY (recipe-runner + extractors, zero AI cost, ~60-90s/poll) for routine collection, vs AI MAPPING (mapper.ts) only when learning a new PMS or repairing a broken selector
- Four extraction modes per feed: csv-download, dom-table, dom-inline, fetch-api (extractors/), chosen by the mapper at learn-time and replayed deterministically
- AI mapper learns 15 feed 'targets' tiered by business value (mapper.ts TARGETS): room status, arrivals, departures, work orders, guests (required) + revenue, rates, channels, forecast, groups, lost&found, activity log (optional)
- Versioned per-PMS-family knowledge files (pms_knowledge_files) with draft/active/deprecated/quarantined lifecycle, DB-enforced exactly-one-active-per-family, learned once per family and shared across all hotels on that family
- Auto-promotion gate (mapping-driver.ts evaluatePromotionGate): a freshly-learned recipe only goes live if all 5 required targets found AND >=3 of 5 business-critical targets found; otherwise parks as draft for admin review or quarantines
- Self-repair 'middle ground' (session-driver maybeFireSelfRepair): after 5 consecutive zero-row polls on one feed, enqueues a CHEAP single-target re-learn (~$2, seeds all other known-good actions so only the broken one is re-mapped) instead of a full $25 re-map; new version hot-reloads within ~60s without restarting the browser
- Knowledge hot-reload (session-driver checkKnowledgeReload): driver polls for a new active knowledge version every 60s and swaps it in-place — no full restart needed when a repair or admin promotion lands
- Multi-layer safety/validation firewall: per-field + cross-field validators (validators.ts, validators-phase2.ts) + descriptor-driven type/range/enum checks (generic-table-writer + pms_table_schemas) run BEFORE every write; bad rows logged to error_logs and dropped per-row without failing the batch
- Three write strategies (generic-table-writer): upsert, append, reconcile (three-way new/update/auto-resolve-disappeared) with a critical safety rule that destructive auto-resolve only runs on FULL snapshots, never deltas, and is scoped to PMS-sourced rows so it can't resolve human-entered tickets
- MFA handling (mfa-handler.ts): proactively clicks 'trust this device / remember me' checkboxes before MFA fires (with sensible default selector patterns across PMS vendors); if MFA actually appears, pauses the hotel to status paused_mfa and surfaces a manual re-login UI rather than trying to automate codes
- Cost controls (cost-cap.ts): $5/hotel/day Claude spend cap with auto-pause and auto-resume at local midnight, plus a separate org-wide daily mapping spend cap ($100 default) to prevent a 300-hotel onboarding wave from cost-bombing; mapping spend tracked separately from the daily operational cap
- Operational guardrails: nightly preventive restart at 3am, RAM-pressure auto-restart (memory-monitor.ts), single-flight read mutex (no overlapping reads), browser-lock so future operator-writes pause polling, supervisor respawn with max 5 restarts/hour dead-letter
- Vision-mapper hardening: per-turn token + wall-clock + per-target step/cost budgets, prompt-caching of system+tools, a Sonnet-based pre/post-screenshot 'critic' that judges whether each click worked (arXiv 2410.00689 pattern), credential placeholders so PMS passwords never enter the Claude conversation, and credential redaction before persisting any error
- Human-in-the-loop assist (human-assist.ts): when the mapper is stuck it can request live help from a watching admin (guidance / mark-unavailable / takeover / abort), with admin-online detection so it doesn't idle-wait, plus a help-flood circuit breaker
- Per-feed real-time downstream push (rules-engine-pinger) so high-priority PMS changes (departures, OOO flips) get a sub-30s reaction instead of waiting for the next cron tick
- Admin operations surface: /admin/property-sessions (per-hotel health + resume/restart/stop), /admin/mfa-resume/[id], doctor checks (cua_sessions_alive, cost_cap_paused, mfa_pending, knowledge_files_active), and a Live Mapping console with progress broadcast

**Strengths:**
- Genuinely novel and defensible architecture: a persistent per-hotel computer-use agent that learns any PMS's UI once and then replays deterministically is a real moat — most competitors integrate via brittle official APIs or per-customer custom scrapers; this generalizes.
- Clean two-speed design (cheap deterministic replay for 99% of operation, expensive AI only for learn/repair) keeps steady-state cost near zero while retaining the flexibility of vision — the right cost/capability tradeoff.
- Exceptional defensive engineering for a solo-founder codebase: nearly every risky decision (fail-open vs fail-safe, idempotency keys, abort-signal propagation, credential redaction, per-row vs per-batch failure, reconcile-only-on-full-snapshot) is explicit and commented with the incident or adversarial-review finding that motivated it.
- Strong cost-runaway protection at three levels (per-target, per-job, per-hotel-per-day, and org-wide-per-day) — the founder clearly internalized that an unbounded vision loop on a broken PMS is the catastrophic financial risk and built real guardrails.
- The knowledge-file versioning + auto-promotion gate + park-as-draft is a mature MLOps-style pattern: bad learnings can't silently go live, rollback is a status flip, and one family's learning benefits all hotels on it.
- Self-repair + hot-reload is elegant: a drifted selector triggers a cheap single-target re-learn that swaps in within ~60s with no browser restart and no full re-map — this is the kind of self-healing most scraping systems never build.
- PII discipline: credentials use placeholders so they never enter the model conversation, screenshots black out password/sensitive fields before the critic sees them, telemetry hashes guest names, and errors are redacted before persistence.
- The validator firewall is real and two-layered, sitting in front of every write, designed specifically around the dangerous failure mode (a successful selector returning stale/wrong data) rather than just crashes.

**Gaps / weak / risky:**
- PROVEN ON ESSENTIALLY ONE PMS. Only Choice Advantage has a seeded knowledge file (0203), only CA has parsers (parsers/ca.ts is the single registered parser family), and template-runner.ts hard-imports '../parsers/ca.js' as the ONLY parser import. A second PMS family would have NO parsers registered and the from-scratch vision mapper would have to both find feeds AND the recipe would reference parser names that don't exist. The 'supports many PMS families' claim is architectural, not yet real.
- The from-scratch vision mapper is the weakest, least-proven, most expensive link — and it's exactly what runs on a brand-new PMS onboarding. Project memory documents it finding only 7/20 targets on a CA full-learn even on Opus, and CA re-maps failing 2x burning ~$11-20. The promotion gate requires all 5 required targets; if the mapper can't reliably find all 5 on a new PMS, onboarding parks-as-draft and needs a human, every time.
- Parser registry is GLOBAL, not family-scoped (parsers/registry.ts is a single Map keyed by parser name). If two PMS families each define a parser with the same name, they collide (the code even warns 'overwriting existing parser'). This is a latent correctness bug the moment a second family is added.
- Hardcoded America/Chicago timezone in cost-cap.ts (cost-cap daily reset / nextLocalMidnight) and memory-monitor.ts (3am nightly restart). Every hotel resets cost and restarts at Central time regardless of its actual location — wrong for a Pacific or Eastern hotel, and the code comments flag per-hotel TZ as an unbuilt future decision (#12).
- restart_count only resets to 0 when a driver successfully reaches 'alive' (session-driver). A hotel that keeps failing login (bad creds, PMS UI change, MFA loop) hits MAX_RESTARTS_PER_HOUR=5 and goes dead-letter with NO automatic recovery and no automatic alert path described — it just stops trying until an admin intervenes. The counter is also never decayed over time.
- Cost cap fails OPEN on read errors (checkBudget returns ok=true if Supabase is flaky). Deliberate to avoid freezing all hotels, but it means a Supabase outage disables the one safety net against a runaway Claude spend loop — the exact scenario the cap exists for.
- The deterministic replay path's robustness depends entirely on selector stability. Self-repair only triggers on a FULL zero-row streak (5 polls = ~2.5 min) per feed; a feed that returns PARTIAL/WRONG data (e.g. a shifted column, a renamed status) would NOT trip zero-row self-repair and could silently write subtly-wrong data caught only by the validators (which check format/range, not semantic correctness).
- 9 of 15 pms_* tables are intentionally empty (guests, revenue, forecast, channels, reports_cache, activity_log, lost&found, groups, rates). The schema promises a rich data moat but most of it is unpopulated — and for CA specifically, franchise tier doesn't expose financials, so those tables may stay empty for the first customer indefinitely.
- Model version drift: code references 'claude-opus-4-7' and defaults to 'claude-sonnet-4-6' throughout (anthropic-client, mapper, usage-log cost table), but project memory says Opus 4.8 is the operational default via per-job override. The cost-tracking table and type unions don't list the model actually being used, so cost attribution and the 'scaleCostCapForModel' logic could be mis-keyed.
- Single-machine-per-hotel topology means no redundancy: if a hotel's Fly machine is down (deploy, crash, region issue), that hotel's data simply stops updating until respawn. The watchdog runs every 5 min, so worst-case staleness is bounded but real, and there's no hot standby.
- Validators reject room numbers not matching /^\d{3,4}[A-Z]?$/ — this is a US-limited-service assumption. A PMS using alphanumeric room codes (e.g. 'PH1', 'A-204', cabin names) would have every room dropped, silently emptying the app. This format assumption is baked into multiple validators.

**Data captured (moat fuel):**
- Per-room status-change history (pms_room_status_log, append-only) — a longitudinal record of how every room flips between clean/dirty/occupied/OOO over time; foundation for cleaning-time analytics and the ML demand/supply models
- Daily housekeeping plan + actuals (pms_housekeeping_assignments) including started_at/completed_at/time_spent — the raw material for per-housekeeper productivity, clean-time standards, and labor optimization
- Arrivals/departures/reservations stream (pms_reservations) with status transitions — drives occupancy, headcount forecasting, and arrival/departure pacing
- Live in-house snapshot per hotel (pms_in_house_snapshot) captured ~every 30s with last-good preservation — a high-frequency operational pulse most PMSes never expose externally
- Work-order / OOO history with reconciliation (pms_work_orders_v2) — maintenance patterns, recurring-room flags, time-to-resolve; feeds CapEx and engineering analytics
- The raw jsonb captured on EVERY pms_* row — a complete, un-mined forensic copy of each PMS row, a latent dataset for auto-discovering new mappable fields and detecting PMS changes
- The pms_knowledge_files themselves are a compounding cross-customer asset: every PMS family mapped once is a reusable integration that future hotels on that family inherit for free — the core defensibility flywheel
- 9 schema tables (guests/loyalty, revenue, forecast, channel performance, rates, groups) defined and ready but currently empty — captured potential rather than captured data today

**Opportunities:**
- Build a 'PMS onboarding cockpit' that turns the from-scratch mapper from a gamble into a guided 30-minute supervised session. Lean hard into the already-built human-assist takeover + Live Mapping console: instead of hoping the vision agent finds all 5 required feeds autonomously, make supervised-takeover the DEFAULT first-onboarding flow for any new PMS family, where an admin watches and one-click-corrects. This converts the weakest link into a repeatable playbook and de-risks the imminent Comfort Suites onboarding.
- Make parsers family-scoped (namespace the registry by pms_family, e.g. key 'choice_advantage:ca_date') before adding a second PMS — small change now, prevents a silent collision later. Pair with a tiny set of GENERIC parsers (generic_date, generic_currency, generic_integer, generic_yn) so a new family can be ~80% covered without bespoke code, matching the 'composable per-family code' goal.
- Add a 'golden snapshot' regression net per hotel: store the last known-good row counts + a fingerprint per feed, and alert (not just self-repair) when counts or shapes drift beyond a band. This catches the PARTIAL/WRONG-data failure mode that zero-row self-repair misses — the dangerous silent-corruption case the validators were explicitly built to guard against but can't fully cover semantically.
- Per-hotel timezone everywhere (cost reset, 3am restart, 'today' derivation). The plumbing already exists (todayInTimezone helper); thread properties' timezone through cost-cap and memory-monitor. Required before any non-Central-time hotel, and trivially improves correctness of 'arrivals remaining today' style counts.
- Turn the dead-letter (failed_restart / >5 restarts) state into an active alert + auto-diagnose: when a hotel goes dead, automatically run the doctor + a one-shot login probe and surface 'likely cause: credentials / PMS UI change / MFA' to the admin, rather than the hotel silently going stale. This is the most likely day-one onboarding failure and right now it's a silent stop.
- Loosen / make-configurable the room-number validator (per-family regex in the knowledge file) so the platform isn't locked to 3-4-digit US room numbers — a cheap change that widens addressable PMSes and prevents a whole-hotel silent-empty failure on an unusual numbering scheme.
- Expose a 'second PMS family' end-to-end as a forcing function: pick one common limited-service PMS (e.g. OPERA Cloud, RoomKeyPMS, Cloudbeds) and run the full learn→promote→replay loop. This is the single highest-leverage thing to prove the defensibility thesis ('works on any PMS') is real, not aspirational — and would surface the registry/parser/validator assumptions above under real load.
- Mine the raw jsonb column already preserved on every pms_* row. Every table keeps the original PMS row in `raw` for forensics — that's a captured, un-mined dataset. A periodic 'schema-discovery' pass over raw could auto-suggest new mappable fields (filling the 9 empty tables) and detect when a PMS started exposing something new, feeding the mapper proactively.
- Add hot-standby or fast-failover for high-value hotels: a second Fly machine in warm standby that can claim a hotel's session within seconds if its primary dies, eliminating the single-machine staleness window for paying customers.
- Cache + cost-bound the critic and consider running it only on LOW-confidence steps (the agent already self-reports uncertainty) — the critic is a per-click Sonnet call and is the kind of thing that quietly dominates mapping cost; gating it would let you raise the per-job cap headroom that the from-scratch mapper actually needs to find buried feeds.

- The from-scratch vision mapper is unproven at finding all 5 required feeds reliably, and Comfort Suites is Choice Advantage — which has a SEEDED knowledge file (0203), so day-one should use the seed, NOT a live vision map. ## ml-service

The ML service (ml-service/, Python FastAPI + XGBoost on Railway) is a genuinely sophisticated 3-layer forecasting + optimization system for housekeeping labor, plus a separate inventory-burn-rate model. Layer 1 (Demand) predicts tomorrow's TOTAL housekeeping minutes for a hotel from room-state composition (checkouts, stayovers day1/day2+, vacant-dirty, occupancy, day-of-week). Layer 2 (Supply) predicts per-(room x housekeeper) cleaning minutes, learning that "room 305 runs +5 min" and "Cindy is faster than Astri." Layer 3 (Optimizer) runs a Monte Carlo simulation that bin-packs predicted room times across H hypothetical housekeepers (Longest-Processing-Time greedy) and returns the SMALLEST headcount where the day finishes on-time with >=95% probability — i.e. "you need 5 housekeepers tomorrow, not 6." A separate inventory model predicts daily burn rate per item and days-until-out for reorder timing.

The headline architectural idea is real and good: a closed-form Bayesian regression (conjugate Gaussian-Inverse-Gamma, t-distributed posterior predictive) that produces calibrated uncertainty bands from N=0 training rows, seeded by cross-hotel "cohort priors" (brand x region x size-tier). So a brand-new hotel gets industry-estimate predictions on day 1, which tighten into hotel-specific predictions as its own cleaning data accumulates (cold-start -> Bayesian fit -> XGBoost graduation at N>=500). There is a serious "honesty contract" throughout: predictions are explicitly labeled fitted vs cold-start vs capacity-unavailable so the UI never calls an industry-estimate an "AI recommendation."

The engineering maturity is high IN THE CORE MATH (correct quantile-inversion sampling, common-random-numbers across headcount candidates, deterministic 128-bit seeding for auditability, advisory locks, atomic install RPCs, shadow-mode promotion with 7-day soak, statistical auto-rollback via paired Wilcoxon + Benjamini-Hochberg FDR). 46 test files. Most of this depth came from many rounds of adversarial review (Codex), and the code comments document a long history of real bugs found and fixed.

BUT the operational reality is much thinner than the code suggests, and there are two pipeline-breaking issues right now (see gaps). The system has likely never run on real data at any scale — Beaumont is the first hotel and onboarding hasn't happened. So every quality claim is theoretical; XGBoost has never activated (its inference path is deliberately disabled), and the models are running on cohort priors / hardcoded industry constants, not learned hotel behavior.

**What it can do today:**
- Demand forecast: predicts a hotel's total housekeeping minutes for tomorrow with uncertainty bands (p10-p95), from room-state composition + occupancy + day-of-week
- Supply forecast: per-(room x housekeeper) cleaning-time predictions that learn individual room quirks and per-housekeeper pace differences
- Headcount optimizer: Monte Carlo + bin-packing returns the minimum number of housekeepers needed to finish tomorrow on-time at a target probability (default 95%), plus 'what if one calls in sick / +5 checkouts' sensitivity
- Inventory burn-rate model: predicts daily usage per item and days-until-out, driving reorder timing and Count-Mode auto-fill once an item 'graduates'
- True cold-start: closed-form Bayesian gives calibrated predictions from zero hotel-specific data, seeded by cross-hotel cohort priors (brand/region/size) — a new hotel is useful on day 1
- Self-improving loop: cold-start -> hotel-specific Bayesian fit -> (intended) XGBoost; weekly retrain, daily inference, shadow-mode promotion with 7-day soak, statistical auto-rollback when a model drifts
- Honesty labeling: every prediction tagged fitted vs industry-estimate vs capacity-unavailable so the UI never overstates confidence
- Operational hardening: per-property advisory locks, deterministic auditable RNG, rate limiting, body-size caps, SQL-injection-safe UUID validation, structured logging, Sentry

**Strengths:**
- Mathematically correct and thoughtfully built core: closed-form Bayesian cold-start, proper quantile-CDF inversion sampling (not the naive uniform-between-quantiles that an earlier version had), common-random-numbers so adjacent headcount options are comparable, deterministic 128-bit seeding for auditability
- Strong safety/operational engineering for a solo project: per-property advisory locks, atomic install-and-activate RPCs, shadow-mode promotion with 7-day soak, statistical auto-rollback (paired Wilcoxon + Benjamini-Hochberg fleet FDR), rate limiting, body caps, SQL-injection-safe UUID validation, structured logging, Sentry, 46 test files
- Genuinely good 'honesty contract': the system goes out of its way NOT to label an industry-estimate as a learned AI recommendation, and omits confidence curves when they'd be meaningless — rare discipline that builds trustworthy UX
- The cold-start + cohort-prior + graduation architecture is the right design for a multi-hotel SaaS and sets up a real network-data-moat story
- Per-(room x housekeeper) supply modeling captures proprietary operational knowledge that competitors and spreadsheets cannot replicate
- Extensive documented bug-history in comments shows the code was hardened by repeated adversarial review rather than written once and forgotten

## comms-voice-agent

Staxis has three distinct AI/communication surfaces, at very different maturity levels.

1) THE AI COPILOT ("Staxis") — the crown jewel and most mature code in this lens. POST /api/agent/command is a real tool-calling agent: it loads the user's role + property, builds a live "hotel snapshot" every turn, retrieves the hotel's long-term memory, hands Claude a role-scoped tool catalog (36 tools), and streams the answer over SSE. It persists every conversation with replay-safety (dangling tool-call cleanup, atomic turn writes), enforces a per-user/day dollar cost cap with atomic reservations, and hardens against prompt injection with explicit trust markers (snapshot=system, tool results=untrusted, memory=reference-only). It reaches the user three ways: the full /chat page, a global "Ask Staxis" bar mounted in AppLayout on every page, and voice. The agent can both ANSWER (occupancy, room status, staff performance, financials, inventory, compliance status, knowledge-hub search) and ACT (mark room clean, reset room, toggle DND, assign room, flag issue, request help, log complaint, log compliance reading/PM check, log found item, create maintenance work order, decide time-off, remember/forget facts, generate schedule, walk-the-user-through a task). Every tool has role + surface gating enforced centrally so a housekeeper can't assign rooms or see financials.

2) VOICE — ElevenLabs Conversational AI wired to the SAME brain via an OpenAI-compatible custom-LLM webhook (/api/agent/voice-brain). Voice shares all system prompts, tools, role checks, and cost caps with text — it's not a separate bot. It's globally mounted (wake-word listener + a voice overlay) and also powers a focused housekeeper "report an issue by voice" mode (tap mic on a room card → speak in EN/ES/Haitian Creole/Tagalog/Vietnamese → one maintenance work order created with verbatim transcript for audit). A separate TTS route (/api/agent/speak, ElevenLabs Jessica) narrates the in-app walkthrough. - Detected-action history (message -> work order/complaint conversions): labeled data for how natural-language staff chatter maps to structured operations
- Nudges + acknowledgment campaign data: which insights managers act on vs ignore, and which staff reliably read/ack announcements — engagement + accountability signal

**Opportunities:**
- UNIFY the two brains: delete the bespoke comms @Staxis assistant and route in-chat questions through /api/agent/command with surface='chat'. Instantly upgrades team-chat AI from 3 tools to 36 with all the hardening, and removes a maintenance fork.
- Turn on real model routing: send cheap classification/read tools to Haiku and reserve Sonnet/Opus for multi-step reasoning. The tiers are already defined — this is mostly making pickModel() actually decide. Likely 30-60% lower agent cost with no UX loss.
- Make the agent DO, not just report. High-leverage new action tools grounded in existing routes: reorder inventory (you already have inventory + alerts), close/resolve a work order, send a staff announcement, send a guest SMS, snooze/escalate a complaint, approve a callout. The plumbing (work orders, comms announce, SMS jobs) already exists.
- Two-way conversational SMS: route inbound /api/sms-reply through the agent brain so a housekeeper can text 'mark 214 clean' or 'I'm sick today' and have it actioned — meeting staff on the channel they already use, no app login needed. The brain, tools, and role-scoping already exist; only the SMS<->agent bridge is missing.
- Guest AI concierge over SMS/WhatsApp: pre-arrival ('what time is breakfast?', 'late checkout?'), in-stay requests (towels -> auto work order/HK task), and post-stay review requests + service recovery. This is a category-defining feature limited-service hotels don't have, and most of the operational tools to fulfill requests already exist.
- Close the proactive loop: let the nudges engine + memory ('room 305's AC fails often') auto-open a recurring-fault work order or draft a vendor message for one-tap approval, instead of only surfacing an insight.
- Lean into the self-learning memory as the moat: surface a 'What Staxis learned about your hotel this week' digest and let the agent proactively apply learned facts (e.g. auto-flag the known slow room block before a busy checkout day).
- Voice 'manager standup': a 60-second spoken daily briefing (occupancy, arrivals/departures, deep-clean queue, open issues, what changed vs yesterday) generated from the snapshot — the agent already has every input.
- Shared multilingual comms is a real strength — extend per-reader auto-translation to inbound guest messages so a front-desk clerk and a Spanish-speaking guest can text in their own languages seamlessly.
- Add agent eval/regression harness coverage for the comms AI helpers (translate/transcribe/polish/detect-action) and a golden-set for the action tools, so the first hotel's real usage doesn't surface silent quality regressions.

**Onboarding risks:**
- Sparse-snapshot day one: the copilot's answers are built from a per-turn live hotel snapshot. With Comfort Suites' PMS feeds just coming online, occupancy/room/staff data will be thin, so the AI may look unhelpful or 'empty' in the first demo unless seeded or framed as warming up.
- Voice depends on ELEVENLABS_API_KEY / ELEVENLABS_VOICE_ID / ELEVENLABS_WEBHOOK_SECRET and the wake-word availability check — if any are unset for the new property's environment, voice silently degrades to text; verify all are configured before onboarding day.
- Voice replay-protection (STAXIS_VOICE_REQUIRE_CONNECTION_BINDING) is OFF by default; fine for launch but it's a known security item to revisit once real voice traffic confirms ElevenLabs forwards conversation_id.
- Inbound SMS only switches language — staff who text the hotel number expecting an answer (a natural instinct) get a generic ack, which can read as 'the AI is broken' during early adoption.
- Two parallel comms paths (the full /communications app vs the SMS-link housekeeper/laundry/engineer pages) risk confusing onboarding — decide and demo ONE primary staff comms flow so the hotel doesn't fragment.
- Working-tree local deletions of agent tool + comms assistant files mean a dev build from the current checkout would fail; ensure the deployed/live code (git HEAD) is what's onboarding, and clean the working tree before any pre-onboarding changes.
- Per-user/day AI cost cap is real and fails-closed; a heavy first-day demo (lots of voice especially, which bills brain + STT + TTS) could trip the cap mid-demo — confirm the cap is sized for the onboarding session.
- Multilingual auto-translation quality in comms is untested at scale; a mistranslated operational message (e.g. a maintenance instruction) during the first real shift is a credibility risk — spot-check the languages the hotel's staff actually speak.
- The copilot's knowledge answers depend on the hotel's Knowledge hub being populated (search_knowledge returns nothing -> 'not documented yet'); if SOPs/vendor contacts aren't loaded before onboarding, the AI will repeatedly say it doesn't know.
